# Company Research Framework and Industry Lenses

Read this file only for deep research, peer comparison, or focused due diligence. Establish the universal company baseline first, then choose one primary industry lens and no more than two secondary lenses. Do not answer every question mechanically; prioritize issues that could change the conclusion.

## 1. Entity resolution and input mapping

### Company name

- Do the brand, legal entity, parent and subsidiaries, domestic and overseas operators, and official website align?
- Are there namesakes, renamings, spin-offs, acquisitions, licensing arrangements, or franchise relationships?
- Is the company currently independent, acquired, inactive, pivoting, or in liquidation?

### Product, app, or brand name

- Who appears on the website, app store, terms of service, privacy policy, copyright notice, and support pages?
- Are the developer, publisher, data controller, merchant of record, and brand owner different entities?
- Which company owns or operates the product, and is it a core revenue source, growth channel, supporting feature, or experiment?
- Could namesake apps, regional versions, white-label products, or imitations create confusion?

### Website

- Is the URL a corporate site, product page, app-store listing, project or repository, or third-party article?
- Do redirects, footers, legal pages, certificates, and official accounts support a consistent entity chain?
- Treat WHOIS, traffic estimates, and search snippets as supporting signals, not standalone proof of ownership or operating status.

## 2. Universal company baseline

### Identity and status

- Can the founding date, headquarters, main operating regions, employee scale, operating status, and listing status be verified?
- What are the legal entity, actual operator, holding company, material subsidiaries, and ultimate controller?
- Is the ownership private, public, state-owned, family-controlled, cooperative, nonprofit, or mixed?

### Team and governance

- Which founders and executives own product, technology, industry operations, supply chain, sales, capital, or regulatory relationships?
- Do their backgrounds match the industry's critical success factors?
- What risks arise from board structure, control, key-person dependence, succession, and incentives?
- Do hiring and management changes indicate expansion, transformation, cost reduction, or operating pressure?

### Products, customers, and value chain

- What are the core portfolio, focus entity, target user, buyer, and value proposition?
- Does the company operate in raw materials, manufacturing, infrastructure, platforms, distribution, brands, retail, or services?
- Which businesses, regions, and customer groups generate revenue, growth, profit, or cash flow?
- How does the company acquire customers, deliver value, provide support, drive renewal or repurchase, and distribute products?

### Capital and operations

- What are the funding history, shareholders, valuation, debt, acquisitions, and capital expenditures? Are figures duplicated or mixed across scopes?
- For public companies, examine revenue, gross margin, operating profit, cash flow, net debt, segments, and customer concentration.
- For private companies, look for pricing, payment paths, contracts, customers, hiring, stores, capacity, transactions, and other verifiable operating signals.
- Which events changed the portfolio, capital structure, regulatory status, ownership, or strategic direction?

## 3. Selecting industry lenses

Classify the business by economic substance rather than marketing labels:

1. **Primary value unit:** software usage, transactions, goods, hardware, asset utilization, professional service, content or intellectual property, financial risk, health outcomes, or energy output?
2. **Primary revenue source:** subscriptions, usage, advertising, commissions, spreads, premiums, merchandise, hardware, licensing, projects, service fees, or rent?
3. **Critical scarce resource:** user network, data, license, distribution, brand, intellectual property, supply chain, capacity, land or assets, talent, or capital?
4. **Primary constraint:** retention, liquidity, inventory, yield, regulation, clinical validation, capital intensity, content supply, or labor leverage?

Choose the lens that best explains the company's main revenue, cost structure, and risk. Add a secondary lens only when a material business line would otherwise distort the conclusion.

## 4. Digital products, software, SaaS, and AI

- Is the product an application, SaaS, API, developer tool, platform, data service, model, infrastructure product, or project-based delivery?
- What are the core job, usage frequency, activation, retention, paid conversion, ARPU or contract value, net revenue retention, and churn?
- Is acquisition product-led, sales-led, channel-led, ecosystem-led, content-led, or bundled? What are CAC, payback, and sales cycle?
- Do costs come mainly from cloud or compute, model APIs, data licensing, human delivery, customer success, or channel fees? Can gross margin improve with scale?
- What are the boundaries of proprietary technology, critical dependencies, data loop, evaluation, reliability, latency, cost, security, compliance, and replacement cost?
- For AI products, distinguish proprietary models, post-training, RAG, agent orchestration, tools, evaluation, data, and ordinary wrappers. Calling a model API is not model development.
- For open-source projects, inspect license, maintainers, commit and release cadence, issues and pull requests, governance, and differences from the commercial offering.

## 5. Consumer internet, content, social, gaming, and mobile apps

- What are DAU and MAU, time spent, session frequency, D1/D7/D30 retention, network density, and geographic mix?
- Are downloads, registrations, active users, paid users, and retention being conflated? Do app-store rankings merely reflect a short campaign?
- Does revenue come from advertising, subscriptions, in-app purchases, live streaming, commerce, licensing, or publishing share? What are ARPU, paid conversion, and platform fees?
- Does growth come from organic sharing, content supply, paid acquisition, preinstallation, distribution partnerships, or cross-product traffic?
- What are the content or intellectual-property supply, creator incentives, moderation, copyright, child-safety, and platform-policy risks?
- For games, add revenue or bookings, payer behavior, LTV, content pipeline, licensing approvals, publishing regions, and dependence on individual hits.

## 6. E-commerce, marketplaces, and local services

- What are GMV, orders, buyers, sellers, frequency, average order value, repeat purchase, take rate, and net revenue?
- How strong are supply and demand acquisition, local density, matching efficiency, fulfillment time, cancellations, returns, and subsidy dependence?
- Does revenue come from commissions, advertising, subscriptions, fulfillment, payments, finance, or first-party retail margin?
- Is the model first-party inventory or a marketplace? What are product quality, counterfeiting, merchant concentration, governance, and cross-border compliance risks?
- Calculate contribution margin after fulfillment, payment, support, refunds, incentives, and acquisition rather than relying on reported gross margin alone.
- Are network effects local, category-specific, or global? Can buyers and sellers easily use multiple platforms?

## 7. Fintech, payments, banking, and insurance

- What are the licenses, regulatory jurisdictions, licensed entities, custody arrangements, and partner financial institutions?
- For payments, examine TPV, take rate, net revenue, chargebacks and fraud, processing cost, and merchant retention.
- For credit, examine originations, loan balance, funding cost, duration, delinquency and loss, vintages, provisions, and capital adequacy.
- For banking and wealth, examine deposits, net interest margin, assets under management, net flows, client assets, and capital requirements.
- For insurance, examine premiums, loss ratio, expense ratio, combined ratio, retention, reinsurance, and reserves.
- Distinguish technology services, distribution, licensed operations, and retained risk. Is growth purchased by accepting underpriced risk?

## 8. Retail, consumer brands, and restaurants

- What are store and channel mix, same-store growth, sales per area, average transaction value, frequency, repurchase, and member contribution?
- What are gross margin, markdowns, returns, inventory turns, sell-through, receivables, payables, and cash conversion cycle?
- Does growth come from openings, price, volume, category expansion, channel shift, or acquisition?
- How do owned stores, franchises, distributors, direct e-commerce, and third-party platforms change revenue recognition and risk?
- Are brand premium, channel control, supply chain, formulation or intellectual property, and consumer mindshare durable?
- For restaurants, add table turns, store format, store-level economics, payback, food safety, and franchise governance.

## 9. Manufacturing, hardware, semiconductors, robotics, and industrials

- Distinguish shipments, end-customer sales, ASP, orders, backlog, capacity, production, and utilization.
- What are bill of materials, manufacturing cost, yield, gross margin, warranty and support cost, and scale effects?
- What is proprietary, outsourced, jointly designed, or purchased? What are the critical suppliers, single-source dependencies, and geopolitical risks?
- What are product cycles, certifications, delivery lead times, inventory and channel inventory, and customer concentration?
- For semiconductors, add process node, design and intellectual property, wafer supply, packaging and testing, design wins, and volume-production timing.
- For robotics, add task success rate, deployed units, utilization, human intervention, maintenance cost, and sales versus robotics-as-a-service economics.

## 10. Healthcare services, medical devices, biotechnology, and pharmaceuticals

- Is the offering a healthcare service, device, diagnostic, drug, platform technology, or research service?
- What are the regulatory pathway, trial stage, primary endpoints, sample size, safety results, approval status, and post-market obligations?
- What are the indication, patient population, clinical value, standard of care, physician adoption, and payment or reimbursement path?
- What are the pipeline, single-asset dependence, patent life, licensing partnerships, milestones, and commercialization rights?
- What are research spending, cash runway, trial cost, manufacturing and quality systems, and commercial capabilities?
- Keep the scientific mechanism, clinical outcome, regulatory approval, and commercial success separate.

## 11. Energy, climate, automotive, and natural resources

- What are reserves, installed or production capacity, output, utilization, project pipeline, and commissioning schedule?
- What are unit cost, yield or conversion efficiency, ASP, long-term contracts, spot-price exposure, and commodity-cycle exposure?
- What are capital expenditures, build cycle, financing structure, subsidies, tax credits, and permit dependencies?
- What are the supply-chain, critical-mineral, recycling, land or grid access, environmental liability, and decommissioning risks?
- For automotive, add deliveries, orders, model mix, ASP, gross margin per vehicle, inventory, distribution, warranty, and software revenue.
- Distinguish technical feasibility, permit approval, secured financing, completed construction, stable production, and commercial profitability.

## 12. Logistics, travel, real estate, and other asset operators

- Is the core asset a fleet, warehouse network, hotel, route, property, land, or infrastructure? What is the owned versus asset-light mix?
- What are utilization, load factor, occupancy, RevPAR, leased rate, revenue per area or mile, and turnover?
- What are fixed and variable costs, capital expenditures, maintenance, lease liabilities, and cycle sensitivity?
- What are orders or bookings, cancellation, seasonality, geographic concentration, asset age, and supply expansion?
- For real estate, add contracted sales, revenue recognition, land bank, net debt, interest coverage, and presale regulation.
- For platform travel or logistics businesses, also apply the marketplace lens and do not treat transaction value as revenue.

## 13. Professional services, education, and labor-intensive businesses

- Does revenue come from projects, billable hours, subscriptions, tuition, success fees, or outsourcing contracts?
- What are backlog, utilization, billable rate, contract value, renewal, delivery cycle, and customer concentration?
- Does margin and growth depend on headcount, or do software, content, process, and brand create operating leverage?
- What are the hiring, attrition, incentive, credential, and key-person risks for critical talent?
- For education, add enrollment, completion, employment or exam outcomes, acquisition, refunds, faculty, and operating or content compliance.
- Distinguish repeatable products, standardized delivery, and bespoke consulting. Do not mistake headcount expansion for software-like scalability.

## 14. Competition, moats, and risks

- Who are the same-layer direct competitors, traditional substitutes, in-house alternatives, and the option to do nothing?
- Why might suppliers, channels, platforms, or customers enter this layer?
- Compare product, pricing, customers, scale, unit economics, assets or technology, regulation, and risk on identical dimensions.
- Under what conditions does the advantage hold? Is it based on network, data, intellectual property, brand, distribution, scale, cost, assets, licenses, or switching costs?
- Which advantages could weaken because of platform policy, technical standards, commodity cycles, regulation, patent expiry, or supply-chain shifts?
- Seek counterevidence such as customer churn, inventory buildup, inadequate yield, clinical failure, subsidy withdrawal, price wars, or single-channel dependence.

## 16. Watch signals

Select three to five indicators that could change the conclusion instead of applying SaaS metrics universally. For each signal, state:

- Current baseline and measurement scope.
- Which conclusion an increase or decrease would support or contradict.
- When it is likely to become observable.
- Which primary source should be monitored.
